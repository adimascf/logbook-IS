    1. Quantitative assessment of insertion sequence impact on bacterial genome architecture
    • They introduced ISseeker to anotate IS element in the draft genome assembly
    • Using genome assembly draft as an input, less computational cost.
    • It identifies IS by blasting their flanking sequences (500 bp) to a reference genome. Both full long contigs includes IS and flanks and and truncated IS (but flanks remain)

    • It doesn’t use ISfinder
    • It does not give us the full inserted sequence, it doesn’t classifiy IS, it doesn’t give functional effect
    • Phylogenetically they suggested that many IS insertion in A. baumannii and K. pneumoniae are conserved.

    • Multiple IS insertion in same region (A. baumannii)
    • Although the genome size is about 30 % smaller than K. pneumoniae genomes (~5.6 Mbp), IS element density from A. baumannii is greater.
    1. Impact of insertion sequences on convergent evolution of Shigella species
    • IS in Shigella genomes account 5-8% of bases, much higher than E.coli with <1%.
    • They used ISMapper to identify insertion sites in chromosomes.
    • Within each Shigella species (S. dysenteriae, S. flexneri, S. sonnei), there are variation in both the number and the location of IS insertion. These IS insertion patterns could be use to differentiate phylogenetic lineages.
    • IS transposition has been ongoing since each species split
    • S. sonnei, S. dysenteriae, and S. flexneri share 53%, 82%, ~33% sites between lineages, respectively. IS Insertion can reflect recent and previous evolution history.
    • Five common IS (IS1, IS2, IS4, IS600, IS911) made upp most insertion in Shigella reference genomes, with IS1 contributed the most. These insertion contributing in convergent evolution of the genus.
    • They measured recent IS activity by counted strain specific insertions divided by strain specific SNVs. S. sonnei shows significantly higher recent activity for all five IS
    • Proportion of pseudogenes associated with IS insertion is 50% in S. sonnei, 22% in S. flexneri and 35% in S. dysenteriae. Significant number of convserved pseudogenes were interupted by IS.
    • Insertions events are less frequent in gene regions (0.20 in S. flexneri, 0.29 in S. sonnei, 0.11 in S. dysenteriae), suggesting purifying selection against insertion in CDS.
    • IS contributed to pathways loss in each species. Disrupting individual metabolic gene or multiple hits within the same pathway. All ancient pathway inactivation events (>80% of genomes) within metabolic reactions were IS insertions in S. sonnei and S. dysenteriae, and 30% in S. flexneri.
    • ISmapper detects IS by mapping paired reads to a list of query IS sequences. Then it finds pairs that only one mapped to the query (unmapped mates correspond to the flanking genomic DNA). These “flanking” reads then map to the reference genome to get insertion location.
    1. Transposases are the most abundant, most ubiquitous genes in nature
    • Using genomes and metagenomes data, they found that transposase gene that produce eznyme that enable movement within part of genome outnumber any other type of genes, including housekeeping genes.
    • Previously labelled as selfish genes with no other purpose than propagating themse;f, the finding that transpoase more abundance that all essential genes, support the idea that these DNA elements play role in host adaptation.
    • This group of mobile element leads to mutation and genome rearrangements that affect diversiciation and adaptation, and drive evolution.
    1. IS26 and the IS26 family: versatile resistance gene movers and genome reorganizers
    • IS26 is found in plasmid and chromosome of pathogens like E. coli and Klebsiella.
    • It is known for good at spreading resistance gene
    • IS26 is 820 bp long with perfectly matching 14 bp TIR. S26 includes a single open reading frame, tnp26, that encodes the 234-aa DDE transposase designated Tnp26, and Tnp26 is required for movement. Movement of IS26 to a new location generates a TSD of 8 bp.
    • Its promoters within the TIRs allow not only expression of its own gene but can also enhance antibiotic resistance gene expression nearby.
    • IS26 forms cointegrates rather than simply inserting a copy itself. The incoming replicon carrying IS26 becomes flanked by two IS26 copies.
      • IS26 has unquie moving mechanism, that enables them spreading resistence genes. It has two distinct cointegration, Pseudo composite transposon (PCT) formation.
    • IS26 enables A. baumannii to capture and integrate resistance genes

  • Compared ISFinder vs ISOSDB

    • ISFinder is a manually curated IS database, the submission requires the researchers to fill out the form. It also provide naming convention for IS.
    • ISOSDB include much larger collection of IS sequences than ISFinder (extended version). They author identified IS element using OASIS from publicly available metagenome and genome. OASIS takes genbank format file (.gbk) as an input and alrady transposases annotated. It has different naming convention with ISFinder (ISOSDB prefix) The IS elements are included in the ISOSDB if meet following criteria:
      • at least two copies of the IS element in a single genome,
      • flanked by terminal IRs
      • it has significant nucleotide homology to an IS element in ISFinder or has a putative transposase.

  • Compared the existing tools

    • OASIS

      • OASIS using annotated transposase from genebank file. It then groups IS into multicopy ISs and single-copy ISs. The edges (or flanking?) of Multicopy ISs defined with homology (maximum likelihood). Edges of single and multicopy (re)define by IR. Identifies partial and missing IS using blast. Then group IS sequeces using hierechial clustering.
      • Classifiy the detected IS sequence using ISfinder database.
      • It doesn’t require the user to provide a reference genome or to provide query IS sequence. The input only gbk file. It relies on the gene (of Tpases) annotation form the genebank file.

    • IS_mapper

      • ISMapper takes as input: a set of paired end Illumina reads, an IS query sequence in fasta format, a referenc genome sequence, in GenBank or FASTA format.
      • The paired end read then are mapped to the IS query, looking for unmapped mate reads, that is reads representing the sequences directly flanking the IS (is it always the case?). The left and right flanking sequences is then mapped to the referenc egenome to find the location of the query IS. Left and right end blocks are extracted, close indiciating (known IS position relative tto the reference), overlaped (novel IS position).

    • ISSeeker

      • ISSeeker takes assembly draft/collection of contigs as an input. The program aligns (using BLAST) collection of IS elemets to the collection of cotigs (assembly draft), looking for full or partial (edges) matches, then it extraxts both flanking regions of that matches (500bp). These flanking (of insetion) region then mapped again to the reference to get the relative position in the genome, infering where the insertion events happened.
      • It attempts to pair left and right flanks that mapped in the same location in the referecne genome to infer a single insertion event.

    • ISEScan

      • The program only use a (draft) genome or metagenome sequence (fasta) as an input. It scan and translate to protein sequence for each enry from the file to identify the transpoase sequences.
      • It uses profile Hidden Markov Models (pHMMs), that is built from Transposase sequence by evolutionary relationshiop, to search remote homolog of transposase sequences from the given fasta.
      • Similiar o OASIS, it groups the IS candidate into single copy and multicopy to identify the edges (boundaries). Then reomve redundants result with hierarchical clustering. Boundaries of a single copy IS is defined by IR, multi copy ISs is determined by homology of those sequences and shared IRs.
      • After idnetifyng transposase ORF, the porgram defines the flanking regions (regions around ORF). It then predicts IRs using local alignment (SW) algorithm to compare left side regions DNA sequences againts the reverse complement of the right side regions. It searches both nearby and farther regions.

    • pseudoR

      • IS detection pipeline that uses ISOSDB
      • It doesn’t rely on paired-end information, instead it uses split reqd approach (looking reads that partially mapped to the assembled genome?).
      • Sequencing reads are first mapped to the assembled contigs. The program then collects all read that are unmapped. These reads the are aligned again to a ISOSDB search for IS terminal ends. If a read contains an IS terminus, that part is deleted, and the rest of the sequence is remapped to the assembled contigs.

    • digIS

      • The program searches for the most conserved part of Tpase (DDE motifs), the catalytic domain.
      • It takes genome (complete?) in fasta as an input, optionally with annotation file gbk. It then translates whole DNA sequence from the input file into protein sequence. Catalyc domain the input sequence is detected using manually curated pHMMs, and the matche regions are tagged as seeds. The seeds are theen extended to get the transaposase sequence of predicted IS.
      • pHMMs are built from MSAa of transposase catalyc domain for each IS family using sequences from ISFinder.

Glossary

  • Lineage: A broad group of bacteria that shares a common evolutionary ancestor.
  • Sublineage: A small group within a lineage that diverge further, because accumulation of mutation, IS insertions, and other genome rearrangements.
  • Inverted repeats (IRs): short DNA sequences at both end of a IS, where the sequence on one end is the reverse complement of the other end (boundaries of the IS). Inverted and complementary.
  • Direct Target Repeats (DTRs): Created during integration, identical sequences (footprint of insertion)
  • iORF: Insertion event of IS in the ORF (genes) that may knock off or regulate the gene expression.
  • Composite Transposon: Two IS in the flanking segement with (resistence) gene in the middle.
  • Target Site Duplications (TSD): short flanking direct repeats (DR, typically ∼3 to 14 bp
  • Gene cassette: Small (0.5-1kb), carry a single gene, often lack a promoter. It can exist as free circular cassette, but only functional when it is inserted into an integron.
  • Integron: It has intl (a site specific recombinase), recombination site, and a promoter for cassete gene expression. They are not mobile by themselves, but embeded in transposons.
  • Unit transposons (Tn): Larger than IS, carry additional genes, such as antibiotic resistance genes.
  • Plasmids: Self replicating extrachromosomal DNA. They often carry multiple MGE including IS, transposon
  • Serotype: groups bacteria/viruses using antigen or toher molecule on the their surfaces
  • Convergent evolution: evolution process that is driven by environment adaptation from distantly related species.
  • Pseudogene: Region that resemble gene structure but inactive due to mutation/IS insertion
  • Pseudo-Compound Transposons: DNA sequence that is flanked by two IS26s in the same direction, carrying antibiotic resistance genes.
  • Putative transposase: Predicted to be transposase (using sequence similarity, motifs, etc), but it’s not experimentally confirmed.
  • Flanking sequence: DNA sequences that adjacent to the IS element, on the left and right ends.
